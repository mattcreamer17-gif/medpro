The observation

MedPro has published briefs on automation bias, black-box reasoning, privacy and security, patient expectations, AI scribes, generative-AI chatbots, and informed consent in AI-assisted care. The content is clear and research-grade. It also lives as PDFs and articles.

Risk officers at your insured health systems read the briefs. Then they call outside counsel to turn the guidance into policy language and vendor-contract redlines. That translation is work MedPro could own. It is the piece between a library and a renewal-stickiness driver.

Meanwhile, the Enterprise Data Warehouse job posting was open through 2025, and the Data Architect role was public. Claims data and risk-content engagement still sit in different systems. The Chief Actuary's question, "how many claims this year touched AI-assisted diagnosis," takes weeks of hand-classification and arrives with error bars wide enough to matter for reserving.

How we would walk the stack

When Eduba looks at a workload, the first question is which layer the problem belongs on. The habit keeps budget off the wrong tool.

60%

Traditional code and database work

The risk-content library is, at root, a database and workflow problem. Tag the briefs. Route them to the right risk officer at the right insured system. Capture what they did with the guidance. Close the loop at renewal.

30%

Rule-based logic

An AI-vendor-contract redline assistant is rule-based language patterns with a light LLM layer on top. The rules carry the MedPro recommended language. The LLM handles variance. Output is a contract a risk committee can actually sign.

10%

Genuine AI problem

A claim-note classifier that surfaces emerging AI-related claim patterns for the Chief Actuary. Free-text claim notes become a structured AI-exposure taxonomy. Reserving questions answered in hours, not quarters.

Teams that skip this distinction pay for an LLM where a Postgres index would do the job faster, cheaper, and with tighter guarantees a Berkshire combined ratio can survive.

Five questions we would bring to the first conversation

  1. When one of your risk officers publishes a new AI brief, how does it actually reach the risk committee at a large insured health system, and is there a feedback loop where the system tells you what they did with it.
  2. How does the claims team tag a new claim that involves AI-assisted diagnosis or an AI-generated scribe note today. Free-text, a controlled vocabulary, or a taxonomy still being built.
  3. When the EDW lands, what is the first question the Chief Actuary wants to ask it about AI-related claim frequency.
  4. The Doctors Company closes its ProAssurance deal this year. Where does MedPro plan to be sharper than the combined entity in the hospital segment by the end of 2026.
  5. If a large hospital system asked you to help them draft their AI vendor contract template, would that come out of risk services, legal, or would you refer it.
